Natural Wine: A Concept So Pure, It Doesn’t Even Exist

By Vitisviniferas

Let’s get one thing straight: there’s no official definition of “natural wine.” It’s the Schrödinger’s cat of the wine world — simultaneously everything and nothing. Ask ten sommeliers what it means, and you’ll get eleven answers, twelve if they’ve had a glass.

In its purest form (pun intended), natural wine is supposed to mean nothing added, nothing taken away. Grapes, yeast, bottle, cheers. No lab-tinkering, no oak flavor pumped in through a syringe, and definitely no 200% new oak barrels blessed by a corporate boardroom.

But lately, the term “natural” has been hijacked. It’s become the badge of honor for cloudy garage projects with more volatile acidity than charm. Some of these so-called wines are basically kombucha in disguise, and we’re all pretending it’s fine because the label is cool and the winemaker has a moustache.

That’s not what natural wine should be.

Let’s talk about the real heroes — the old-school rebels who were making wine this way before it was a hashtag. Enter: The Gang of Four from Beaujolais. Marcel Lapierre, Jean Foillard, Guy Breton, and Jean-Paul Thévenet. These guys weren’t chasing trends — they were reviving the soul of Gamay, returning to the teachings of Jules Chauvet (the OG natural wine scientist-poet).

Their wines? Lively, soulful, expressive — without ever smelling like a barnyard mishap. Clean, but unfiltered. Fresh, but deep. Proof that you don’t need flaws to make something honest.
